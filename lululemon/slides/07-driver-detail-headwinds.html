Three structural headwinds compound the pressure on premium activewear brands in Australia.

Synthesis
Verified
01 · Headwind

Ultra-fast fashion incursion.

AUD 3B+ annual
Estimated SHEIN and Temu annual revenue in Australia. Most low-value imports enter tax-free under existing AU customs rules.

Australians are among the highest per-capita consumers of fast fashion globally. SHEIN and Temu have leveraged lenient AU import tax rules to flood the market with ultra-low-cost activewear alternatives, depressing category average selling prices and accelerating consumer price sensitivity.

While Lululemon’s premium technical positioning provides meaningful insulation, the volume effect creates a structural headwind on consumer expectations around value. New customer acquisition at Lululemon’s price points is harder in an environment where comparable-looking product is available at a fraction of the cost.

Source: texfash.com SHEIN, Temu and the tariff fallout analysis, 2025.
02 · Headwind

Heightened price sensitivity and discretionary spending contraction.

68.5
Australian consumer confidence index, March 2026. Only 15% of Australians report being financially better off than a year ago.

Consumer confidence at 68.5 (March 2026) and elevated goods inflation at 5.5% year-on-year are compressing discretionary spend. Approximately 86% of consumers now prioritise price in purchasing decisions, and 75% self-describe as active bargain hunters.

For Lululemon, charging AUD 100 to AUD 200-plus per item, this creates real headwinds in acquiring new price-sensitive consumers. Existing loyal customers are more resilient, but the top-of-funnel acquisition challenge is material, particularly in a market tracking peer UK and US softness in Lululemon comparable sales.

Verified
Source: ABS CPI Australia March 2026; vocal.media AU Activewear Market 2026.
03 · Headwind

Sustainability demand without credible delivery.

501M kg/yr
Annual textile waste discarded in Australia. The Australian Fashion Council’s Seamless initiative targets a circular clothing industry by 2030 via a 4-cent levy per garment.

Growing consumer and regulatory pressure on sustainability is raising the bar for brands across all apparel categories. Lululemon has a stated Be Well impact agenda including carbon neutrality and circular product design, but its circular programs, notably the Like New re-commerce initiative, are inconsistently available in Australia versus North America.

Brands without credible, verifiable sustainability delivery face increasing reputational exposure. For Lululemon AU, this is a gap between global positioning and local execution that a competitor could exploit in brand preference studies.
